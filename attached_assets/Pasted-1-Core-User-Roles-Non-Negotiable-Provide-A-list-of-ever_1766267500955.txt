1. Core User Roles (Non-Negotiable)

Provide:

A list of every role that can log into the CRM
Example:

Intake staff

Vet tech

Behavior team

Foster coordinator

Adoption counselor

Shelter admin

Volunteer

Why this matters:
CRMs feel bloated when one screen tries to serve multiple roles.
My first pass will be to determine:

Which roles should never see certain data

Which roles need only 3–5 actions per day

Which roles deserve their own simplified “mode”

If a role cannot be described in one sentence, it is already over-scoped.

2. “Day in the Life” for Each Role (Critical)

Provide for each role:

Top 3 things they must do every day

Top 2 things that are occasional

What blocks them from finishing their shift on time

Example format (short is fine):

Role: Intake Staff
Daily:
- Log new dogs
- Assign kennel
- Flag medical issues
Occasional:
- Owner surrender paperwork
- Bite history review
Pain points:
- Too many required fields
- Unsure when a dog is “ready” to move forward


Why this matters:
This exposes feature clutter immediately.
Anything not tied to a daily or occasional task is either:

Admin-only

Automation-eligible

Or should not exist yet

3. Your Current Navigation Map (Even Rough)

Provide one of the following:

Sidebar menu list

Top-level routes (e.g. /intake, /pipeline, /medical, etc.)

Or a screenshot with labels

Why this matters:
CRMs feel overwhelming when:

Navigation reflects engineering structure instead of mental models

Too many “entry points” exist for the same task

I will:

Collapse redundant sections

Identify false distinctions (e.g. “Medical” vs “Health”)

Recommend a single operational spine

4. Your Pipeline Stages (Exact Names)

Provide:

Every pipeline column name, in order

Any conditional logic (locks, blockers, approvals)

Example:

Intake → Stray Hold → Medical Eval → Behavior Review → Ready → Listed → Adopted


Why this matters:
Pipelines often become feature dumping grounds.

I will evaluate:

Which stages are actually states vs tasks

Which should be automated transitions

Which should never be manually draggable

A clean pipeline alone can remove 30–40% perceived complexity.

5. Required Fields vs Optional Fields

Provide:

A list of fields required at intake

Fields required before “Ready for Adoption”

Fields that are “nice to have”

Why this matters:
This is where burnout hides.

Most CRMs fail because:

They require too much data too early

They confuse data completeness with operational readiness

I will help you define:

Progressive disclosure

Auto-fill candidates

“Good enough to move forward” rules

6. What You’re Personally Uncomfortable Cutting

Provide (honestly):

Features you like but suspect are too much

Features you added “because other CRMs have them”

Anything you are emotionally attached to

Why this matters:
Every great product has a graveyard of good ideas.

I will not remove value—only sequence it correctly.

What I Will Deliver Back to You

Once you provide the above, I will give you:

Feature Kill / Keep / Defer list

Role-based surface area map (who sees what)

Simplified operational spine (the 1–2 screens that matter)

“This feels calm” UX principles specific to shelter stress environments

A clear answer to:
“Is this too much—or just badly staged?”
